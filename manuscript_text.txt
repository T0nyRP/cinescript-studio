Chapter 1: The Recruitment
Chapter 1: The Recruitment
==================================================
Three Months Earlier
At 3:47 AM, campus security found Jace Maddox in Professor Harrison's laboratory,
surrounded by $2.3 million worth of destroyed equipment. The prototype autonomous
navigation system lay in pieces across three workstations.
Officer Rodriguez surveyed the wreckage. "Jesus Christ, Maddox. What did you do?"
Jace didn't look up from his laptop, which was connected to what remained of the
navigation system through a tangle of cables. His blond hair hung in greasy strands across
his forehead, and the bags under his blue eyes suggested he hadn't slept in at least thirtysix hours. His t-shirt bore coffee stains, his jeans were torn at the knee, and his sneakers
were untied.
He looked exactly like what he was: a genius having a breakdown.
"I improved it." Jace's voice was hoarse from lack of sleep and too much caffeine. His
fingers never stopped moving across the keyboard. "The original code had a fatal flaw in
the threat assessment protocol. In a real combat scenario, it would've gotten soldiers
killed."
Rodriguez moved closer, careful not to step on any of the expensive debris. "By blowing it
up?"
"By stress-testing the decision-making algorithms under extreme conditions." Jace finally
looked up, and his eyes held that manic intensity Rodriguez had seen before in students
who'd pushed themselves too far. "The system couldn't handle ethical paradoxes. I gave it
one. I had to know if it would fail."
"And did it?"
"Spectacularly." Jace gestured at his laptop screen, which showed cascading error
messages and diagnostic readouts that meant nothing to Rodriguez. "I presented the
system with a no-win scenario—save the mission objective or save civilian lives. Standard
trolley problem, scaled up to military operations. The AI froze for seventeen seconds, then
started running in logic loops. In the field, that delay would be catastrophic."
Rodriguez keyed his radio. "Control, you're gonna need someone from the Dean's office.
And probably maintenance. Lots of maintenance."

Jace stared at his screen, his expression mixing triumph and despair. "Nobody else was
testing for this. Harrison was so focused on processing speed and accuracy that he forgot
about the moral dimensions. What good is a system that makes decisions faster if it
makes the wrong decisions?"
"Kid, I don't think the Dean's gonna care about your philosophical reasoning when he sees
this mess."
"He should care." Jace's voice rose slightly, passion breaking through exhaustion. "This
system was going to be deployed in war zones. Real soldiers were going to trust their lives
to code that couldn't handle basic ethical dilemmas. I didn't destroy it—I proved it was
already broken."
Officer Sarah Kim arrived three minutes later, followed shortly by a very angry Dean
Morrison and an even angrier Professor Harrison. The professor stood in the doorway of his
destroyed laboratory, his face cycling through shades of red that suggested his blood
pressure was reaching dangerous levels.
"Maddox." Harrison's voice shook with barely controlled rage. "You brilliant, arrogant,
catastrophically stupid child. Do you have any idea what you've done?"
Jace finally stood up, swaying slightly from exhaustion. He was tall—six foot four—and thin
in the way of people who forget to eat when they're focused on something important. "I
proved your system was fundamentally flawed, Professor. You should be thanking me."
"THANKING YOU?" Harrison's voice echoed through the laboratory. "You've destroyed two
years of work and $2.3 million in Defense Department funding! You've probably ended my
career and definitely ended yours!"
Dean Morrison stepped between them, his expression grim. "Mr. Maddox, you are under
arrest for destruction of federal property. Officer Rodriguez, please place him in
handcuffs."
Rodriguez had done this part before, but it never got easier. Jace didn't resist as the cuffs
clicked around his wrists, but his eyes never left Professor Harrison's face.
"Sir," Jace said quietly, "I know you spent years on this project. I know you believe in it. But
it wasn't ready. In its current state, it would've been dangerous. I'm sorry about your lab,
but I'm not sorry about exposing the flaw."
Harrison's anger seemed to deflate slightly, replaced by something that might have been
grudging respect. "The problem, Mr. Maddox, is that you didn't expose the flaw through
proper channels. You didn't write a paper or schedule a presentation. You broke into my
laboratory in the middle of the night and destroyed government property. Good intentions
don't excuse criminal behavior."

"Sometimes they should," Jace said, but his voice lacked conviction. He was starting to
realize the full magnitude of what he'd done.
==================================================
Two hours later, Jace sat in handcuffs in the campus security office. Dean Morrison read
from his file: "IQ 187. Full MIT scholarship. Two published papers on AI ethics before your
eighteenth birthday." He closed the file with a snap. "And you threw it all away."
The preliminary damage assessment sat between them: $2.3 million in destroyed
equipment, federal contract breach, potential criminal charges carrying eight to ten years
in prison.
"The system was flawed," Jace said quietly. "Someone had to prove it."
"Someone did. You. Congratulations." Morrison's voice carried the weight of
disappointment. "Mr. Maddox, you are hereby expelled from MIT, effective immediately.
The university will be pressing charges for destruction of federal property. Your scholarship
is revoked, your access to all MIT facilities is terminated, and you are banned from campus
permanently."
The words hit Jace like physical blows. MIT had been his home for three years, the first
place where his intelligence hadn't been a burden or a freakish talent to be exploited. Here,
being brilliant was normal. Expected. Celebrated.
And now it was gone.
"Can I say something?" Jace asked.
"You can say it to your lawyer."
"I don't have a lawyer."
Morrison's expression softened slightly—the first hint of human sympathy since this
disaster began. "Then I suggest you get one. The charges are serious, Jace. Federal
property, Defense Department contract violations, potential national security implications.
You're looking at eight to ten years in federal prison."
The door opened, and Officer Rodriguez entered. "Dean Morrison, there's a gentleman here
to see Mr. Maddox. Says he's from the government."
Morrison frowned. "What kind of government?"
"He didn't specify. But he has federal credentials and a very polite way of suggesting that
we let him talk to the prisoner."
Jace and Morrison exchanged confused glances. It was too early for federal prosecutors to

be involved, and the FBI typically didn't move this fast on property destruction cases.
"Send him in," Morrison said.
The man who entered the office was tall and silver-haired, probably in his early sixties, with
the kind of bearing that suggested either military background or decades in law
enforcement. His suit was expensive but understated, his shoes polished to a mirror shine,
and his blue eyes were sharp enough to cut glass. He carried himself with the weight of
someone who'd made difficult decisions for years and learned to live with the
consequences.
"Dean Morrison." He extended his hand. "Marcus Kane. I represent certain government
interests."
"What kind of interests?"
"The classified kind." Kane turned to Jace. "Mr. Maddox, I've been reading about your
adventure in Professor Harrison's laboratory. Impressive work."
"I destroyed $2.3 million in government equipment," Jace said. "That doesn't usually
qualify as impressive."
"It does when you expose critical flaws in systems that are about to be deployed in combat
zones." Kane pulled out a tablet and showed them both a document heavy with redacted
sections and classification stamps. "Professor Harrison's navigation system was scheduled
for field testing in Afghanistan next month. If it had failed the way you predicted, American
soldiers would've died."
Morrison leaned forward. "Are you saying Maddox was right about the flaws?"
"I'm saying that preliminary analysis of the code he extracted before destroying the
hardware reveals exactly the kind of ethical decision-making failures he identified. The
Defense Department is very interested in understanding how a nineteen-year-old student
identified problems that our entire quality assurance team missed."
Jace felt a flicker of vindication, but it was quickly overwhelmed by confusion. "Who are
you, really?"
"Someone who appreciates talent, Mr. Maddox. Someone who understands that
conventional rules don't always serve the greater good." Kane closed his tablet. "Dean
Morrison, I'm here to request custody of your prisoner."
"On what grounds?"
"National security. Mr. Maddox has information about Defense Department systems that
requires immediate debriefing. My organization is prepared to handle the criminal charges
through alternative channels."

Morrison looked between Kane and Jace, clearly trying to process what was happening.
"Your organization. Which is?"
"Classified." Kane's smile didn't reach his eyes. "But I can assure you that Mr. Maddox will
be dealt with appropriately. The question is whether you want to help us or hinder us."
It wasn't really a question, and everyone in the room knew it. Whatever organization Kane
represented, they had enough pull to walk into MIT and commandeer a federal prisoner
without breaking a sweat.
"Fine," Morrison said finally. "But I want it on record that MIT cooperated fully with federal
authorities."
"It will be noted." Kane turned to Officer Rodriguez. "Remove the handcuffs, please."
As the cuffs came off, Jace rubbed his wrists and looked at Kane with a mixture of
suspicion and curiosity. "What happens now?"
"Now we talk. Outside, preferably. I find that difficult conversations are easier when they're
not conducted in security offices." Kane headed for the door. "Come along, Mr. Maddox.
Your old life is over. Let's discuss what comes next."
==================================================
They walked to the Charles River in silence. Dawn was breaking, and a crew team was
practicing on the water.
"Tell me about your family," Kane said.
"My father died when I was twelve. My mother remarried someone who resented having a
genius stepson. I left at sixteen for MIT." Jace kicked at a pebble. "Why does this matter?"
"Because what I'm offering will require you to disappear completely. New identity, new life.
Jace Maddox will cease to exist."
"Sounds like witness protection."
"Similar concept, different application." Kane pulled out a tablet showing a holographic
neural network—layers of AI architecture woven through human brain mapping.
"This is SYNAPSE," Kane said. "Direct neural interface technology. Human consciousness
merged with artificial intelligence. Processing speed increased tenfold. Perfect memory.
Real-time data analysis. Reflexes that border on precognition."
"You're talking about cyborgs."

"We prefer 'enhanced operatives.'"
Jace studied the diagram, his exhaustion forgotten. The architecture was elegant and
terrifying. "How do you prevent immune rejection?"
"Biocompatible nano-materials smaller than red blood cells, coated to mimic natural
tissue. The real breakthrough was the software—creating an AI that could integrate with
human consciousness without overwhelming it."
"How many test subjects have you had?"
Kane's expression didn't change, but Jace caught a flicker of something—regret, maybe, or
the weight of having sent seventeen people into surgery knowing the odds. "Seventeen
previous subjects. Three died in surgery. Eight experienced rejection. Four had
psychological integration failures. Two achieved integration—one operational, one
catastrophic failure where the AI took complete control."
"What happened to them?"
"Alive, but the original personality is gone."
Jace felt a chill run down his spine. "And you want me to volunteer for this?"
"I want you to be our eighteenth test subject. But more than that—I want you to help us
understand why the previous attempts failed." Kane started walking again, this time along
the riverbank path. "You have something our other subjects lacked: a deep understanding
of AI ethics and decision-making architecture. You understand the philosophical
implications of merging human and artificial consciousness."
"Understanding the implications doesn't mean I can prevent catastrophic failure."
"No, but it means you'll recognize the warning signs before it's too late. You'll know when
the AI is overstepping its boundaries, when the integration is becoming unstable." Kane
stopped at a bench overlooking the water and sat down, gesturing for Jace to join him. "The
previous subjects were all soldiers, intelligence operatives, people trained to follow orders
and complete missions. They were excellent at physical skills, but they lacked the
theoretical framework to understand what was happening to them on a cognitive level."
"So you want someone who can think their way through the integration process."
"Exactly. Someone who can maintain their sense of self even as they're being augmented
by artificial intelligence. Someone who can work with the AI instead of being controlled by
it." Kane leaned back on the bench, looking suddenly older and more tired. "We've invested
two billion dollars in the SYNAPSE project over the past eight years. We've made incredible
breakthroughs in human-AI integration. But we're at a critical juncture—either we find a
way to achieve stable long-term symbiosis, or the project gets shut down." His voice carried
the weight of someone who'd fought countless bureaucratic battles to keep the program

alive.
"And seventeen people will have died or been permanently damaged for nothing."
"Eighteen, if you accept and it fails." Kane turned to look at Jace directly. "I won't lie to
you—this is dangerous. The surgery alone carries significant risks. The integration process
is unpredictable. And even if everything goes perfectly, you'll be fundamentally changed.
You won't be the same person you are now."
"I'm already not the same person I was yesterday," Jace said quietly. "MIT expelled me. I'm
facing federal charges. My entire future just evaporated."
"Which brings us to the alternative." Kane's voice took on a harder edge. "Federal prison.
Eight to ten years, minimum security if you're lucky. By the time you get out, you'll be in
your thirties. The field will have moved on without you. You'll be a convicted felon with a
brilliant mind and nowhere to use it."
"So it's not really a choice."
"It's absolutely a choice. You can choose prison and safety, or you can choose risk and
purpose." Kane stood up from the bench. "What you did in Professor Harrison's laboratory—
destroying that navigation system because you believed it was flawed—that took courage. It
also took arrogance, recklessness, and a complete disregard for consequences. Those are
all qualities we need in an enhanced operative."
"Arrogance and recklessness are qualities you need?"
"When they're balanced by intelligence and moral clarity, yes. ORION doesn't need perfect
soldiers. We need people who can make impossible decisions in impossible situations.
People who will do the right thing even when the right thing violates orders, protocols, and
common sense."
Jace stood up and walked to the edge of the riverbank, watching the crew team continue
their practice. The rowers moved in perfect synchronization, eight individuals functioning as
a single unit. That's what Kane was offering—a chance to become something greater than
himself, a merger of human and machine that could accomplish things neither could
achieve alone.
But at what cost?
"If I do this," Jace said slowly, "and if the integration succeeds—what then? What would
ORION expect from me?"
"Missions that require capabilities beyond normal human limits. Infiltration operations in
hostile territory. Intelligence gathering in environments where conventional agents can't
operate. Neutralization of threats that represent existential risks to national security." Kane
joined him at the riverbank. "The world is changing, Jace. Technology is advancing faster

than our ability to control it. Hostile nations and terrorist organizations are developing
weapons that could destabilize global civilization. We need enhanced operatives who can
counter those threats."
"Enhanced operatives. That's a polite way of saying enhanced killers."
"Sometimes. But more often it means enhanced analysts, enhanced diplomats, enhanced
problem-solvers." Kane's expression was serious. "ORION was established in 1987 with a
single mandate: develop capabilities that our enemies can't anticipate, can't counter, and
can't understand. For thirty-eight years, we've been working on human enhancement
protocols. SYNAPSE represents the culmination of that research."
The sun had fully risen now, bathing the Charles River in golden light. Across the campus,
students would be waking up, getting ready for classes, living their normal lives. Jace would
never have that again, regardless of which choice he made.
Prison meant safety but stagnation. SYNAPSE meant risk but purpose.
"When do we start?" Jace asked.
Kane smiled, and this time it reached his eyes. "Right now. We have a plane waiting at
Hanscom Air Force Base. By tonight, you'll be in Colorado beginning the first phase of
integration."
"And if I change my mind?"
"Then you can walk away, go back to facing the federal charges, and take your chances
with the legal system." Kane extended his hand. "But something tells me you won't change
your mind. You've already made your choice—you just needed to hear yourself say it out
loud."
Jace shook Kane's hand, sealing a bargain that would change everything about who and
what he was.
==================================================
A black SUV waited outside. As they drove toward Hanscom Air Force Base, Jace watched
MIT disappear in the rearview mirror. Three years ago, he'd arrived as a sixteen-year-old
genius with nothing. He was leaving as a criminal facing prison or transformation.
Some might call that trading one prison for another.
But for the first time in years, Jace felt hope.
Maybe this wasn't the end. Maybe it was the beginning.
==================================================

Hanscom Air Force Base was located in Bedford, Massachusetts, about twenty minutes
from MIT. The SUV bypassed the main entrance and headed straight for a private airfield
where a sleek corporate jet waited with engines already running.
"No wasting time," Jace observed.
"Time is the one resource we can't manufacture," Kane replied. "Every hour you spend in
legal limbo is an hour the Defense Department's lawyers are building their case. Better to
get you out of their jurisdiction before they decide you're too valuable to let go."
The jet's interior was luxurious but functional—leather seats, polished wood trim, and
enough electronic equipment to run a small intelligence operation. Two other people were
already aboard: a woman in her fifties with the bearing of a senior military officer, and a
younger man who looked like he'd been pulled from a tech startup, complete with hornrimmed glasses and a hoodie.
"Jace Maddox," Kane said, "meet Colonel Sarah Briggs, ORION's director of field
operations, and Dr. Michael Zhang, lead programmer for the SYNAPSE artificial intelligence
architecture."
Briggs stood and extended her hand. Her grip was firm, her eyes assessing. "Mr. Maddox.
I've read your file. Impressive academic record, equally impressive record of
insubordination and property destruction."
"I prefer to think of it as problem identification."
"In ORION, we call it tactical flexibility. You'll fit right in." She gestured to a seat. "Strap in.
We've got four hours to bring you up to speed on what you've agreed to."
Dr. Zhang didn't bother with handshakes. He was already engrossed in a laptop covered
with code that Jace recognized as advanced neural network architecture. "Seventeen
previous integration attempts," he said without looking up. "Three deaths, eight rejections,
four psychological catastrophes, one ongoing success, and one catastrophic failure. The
statistical probability of your successful integration is approximately thirty-seven percent."
"Those are terrible odds."
"They're better than they were six months ago. We've refined the integration protocols
based on previous failures." Now Zhang did look up, and his eyes held the intensity of
someone who'd spent too many sleepless nights staring at problems that refused to be
solved. "The key issue is maintaining equilibrium between human consciousness and
artificial intelligence. Too much AI influence, and you get a personality takeover. Too little,
and the enhancement benefits are minimal."
The jet taxied toward the runway, and Kane settled into the seat across from Jace with a
thick folder marked "TOP SECRET - SYNAPSE PROJECT - COMPARTMENTED INFORMATION."

"Before we begin," Kane said, "I need you to understand something fundamental about
ORION and about the SYNAPSE program specifically. What we're doing exists in a legal and
ethical gray zone. The technology we've developed could be used for tremendous good—
helping paralysis victims regain mobility, treating traumatic brain injuries, enhancing
human cognitive abilities. But it could also be used for control, manipulation, and the
creation of soldiers who follow orders without question or moral hesitation."
"Which is it?" Jace asked. "Good or control?"
"Both. Neither. It depends entirely on who's using it and why." Kane opened the folder to
reveal technical diagrams, surgical procedures, and dense medical documentation.
"ORION's mandate is national security, but that mandate is interpreted by human beings
with human flaws. We try to operate ethically, but we're not always successful."
Colonel Briggs leaned forward. "What Director Kane is trying to say, in his typically
diplomatic way, is that you're about to become a weapon. A very sophisticated, very
expensive weapon. How that weapon gets used is partially up to you, but ultimately
controlled by people who may not share your values or your ethical concerns."
"So why tell me this now?" Jace asked. "Why not wait until after the surgery when I'm
already committed?"
"Because we've learned from our failures," Dr. Zhang said, finally closing his laptop. "The
subjects who experienced catastrophic integration failure were all people who didn't fully
understand or accept what was being done to them. They thought they were just getting
upgrades—faster reflexes, better memory, enhanced perception. They didn't understand
that they were fundamentally changing what it meant to be human."
Kane pulled out several documents from the folder. "These are your non-disclosure
agreements. Read them carefully. They cover everything from the existence of ORION itself
to the specific technical details of the SYNAPSE program. Violating any of these
agreements carries a minimum sentence of twenty-five years in federal prison, and that's if
we prosecute through official channels."
"And unofficially?"
"Unofficially, people who violate ORION security protocols tend to disappear." Kane's
expression didn't change, but the threat was clear. "We're offering you an extraordinary
opportunity, Jace, but it comes with extraordinary responsibilities."
The jet reached the runway and began its takeoff roll. As they lifted off, Jace looked out the
window at Massachusetts disappearing below him—MIT, Cambridge, Boston, the entire life
he was leaving behind.
He began reading the non-disclosure agreements.

The agreements were comprehensive: ORION's existence, SYNAPSE technology, surgical
procedures, mission protocols. Seventeen contracts that covered every aspect of his new
life.
"I'm signing away everything," Jace said.
"You did that when you destroyed the navigation system," Briggs replied. "This way, at least
you get something in return."
Dr. Zhang opened his laptop again and turned it toward Jace. The screen showed a 3D
model of a human brain with thousands of tiny points of light scattered throughout the
neural tissue. "Each of those lights represents a SYNAPSE node—a microscopic implant
that interfaces directly with your neurons. There are approximately twelve thousand nodes
in a full integration, strategically placed throughout your brain's cognitive centers."
"Twelve thousand brain implants?" Jace felt queasy.
"Each one smaller than a red blood cell. The surgery takes fourteen hours, and it's
performed by robotic systems with precision measured in microns." Zhang zoomed in on
one section of the brain. "The nodes form a distributed network that can communicate with
each other and with a central processing unit implanted in your temporal lobe. Together,
they create a secondary neural pathway that runs parallel to your organic brain."
"A second brain."
"More like an AI partner that shares your skull. The SYNAPSE system learns from your
thought patterns, adapts to your cognitive style, and eventually becomes so integrated that
you won't be able to tell where your thoughts end and its calculations begin."
Kane handed Jace a pen. "Sign the agreements, and we move forward. Refuse, and we
turn this plane around and you face the consequences of your actions at MIT."
Every choice had led him here: his father's death, his stepfather's cruelty, MIT, Harrison's
lab.
He signed the agreements.
All seventeen of them.
"Welcome to ORION," Kane said, collecting the signed documents. "Now let's talk about
what's going to happen to you over the next six weeks."

Chapter 2: The Facility
Chapter 2: The Facility
==================================================
The flight to Colorado took four hours, but it felt like four minutes.
Jace spent the entire time absorbing information about ORION, SYNAPSE, and the world he
was entering. Colonel Briggs walked him through the organizational structure—or at least
the parts she was authorized to reveal. Dr. Chen explained the technical architecture of the
neural implants in language that somehow managed to be both highly technical and
deeply unsettling.
But it was Director Kane who provided the most important briefing: the philosophy behind
ORION's existence.
"We were established in 1987," Kane said, pulling up classified documents on a tablet.
"The Cold War was ending, but new threats were emerging. Terrorism. Cyber warfare.
Asymmetric conflicts that conventional military forces couldn't address. The government
needed something else—a black operations division with capabilities our enemies couldn't
anticipate or counter."
"And nobody knows you exist?" Jace asked.
"The President knows. The Director of National Intelligence knows. A handful of senior
senators and congresspeople on the intelligence committees know, though they'd deny it
under oath." Kane scrolled through organizational charts. "Total operational personnel:
approximately four hundred people worldwide. Annual budget: classified, but in the billions.
Success rate: classified, but significant."
"How do you measure success in an organization that doesn't officially exist?"
Colonel Briggs smiled grimly. "By counting the catastrophes that don't happen. The terrorist
attacks that never occur. The wars that never start. ORION's greatest achievements are the
things you never hear about on the news."
The plane began its descent, and Jace looked out the window at the Colorado landscape
below. Mountains stretched in every direction, covered in pine forests and crowned with
snow-capped peaks. Somewhere down there was the facility where his transformation
would begin.
"Where exactly are we landing?" he asked.
"Classified location," Kane said. "For the purposes of any communication with the outside

world—not that you'll be having much—you're at a Defense Department research facility
studying advanced materials engineering."
"And the truth?"
"The truth is seven levels underground in a mountain that doesn't appear on any civilian
maps."
The plane touched down on what appeared to be a private airstrip carved into a valley
between two peaks. As they taxied toward a hangar, Jace could see that the entire facility
was designed to be invisible from above—camouflaged buildings, vehicle entrances hidden
under natural rock formations, even the runway painted to look like a dried riverbed from
aerial reconnaissance.
"Welcome to nowhere," Kane said as they deplaned. "Population: classified. Mission:
classified. Existence: classified."
A black SUV waited beside the hangar, and within minutes they were driving through
winding mountain roads that seemed to lead nowhere. The driver—a young woman in
civilian clothes who didn't introduce herself—navigated switchbacks and narrow passes
with the confidence of someone who'd made this trip hundreds of times.
"How far?" Jace asked.
"Twenty-seven point three miles," the driver said. It was the only thing she said during the
entire trip.
The facility's entrance was hidden behind what appeared to be a research station for
wildlife management—complete with official-looking signs, a small parking lot with a few
civilian vehicles, and even a gift shop that looked convincingly abandoned. But when the
SUV pulled around to the back of the building, a massive door opened in what Jace had
thought was solid rock.
They drove into the mountain itself.
The tunnel was wide enough for two vehicles side by side and descended at a gentle angle,
lit by LED strips embedded in the walls. Every hundred yards or so, Jace could see what
looked like blast doors—thick steel barriers that could seal off sections of the tunnel in an
emergency.
"Security protocols," Colonel Briggs explained, noticing his interest. "If the facility is ever
compromised, we can isolate individual levels and sections. Makes it harder for enemies to
penetrate to the core operations."
After what felt like miles of driving through the mountain, they arrived at a checkpoint
where armed guards in unmarked uniforms scrutinized their credentials. The security here
was different from anything Jace had seen—retinal scanners, palm print readers, and what

looked like some kind of electromagnetic scanning system that probably detected
weapons and electronic devices.
"Mr. Maddox," one of the guards said, consulting a tablet. "You're flagged as a new arrival.
I'll need to take your photo, fingerprints, and DNA sample for the permanent security
database."
The process took fifteen minutes and felt more invasive than any airport security screening.
When it was finally complete, they were waved through to an elevator that looked like it
belonged in a luxury hotel rather than a secret underground facility.
"Seven levels," Kane said as the elevator descended. "Level One is administration and
command operations. Level Two is training facilities. Level Three is medical and research.
Level Four is residential quarters. Level Five is specialized equipment and armory. Level Six
is classified even from most ORION personnel. Level Seven is SYNAPSE integration and
testing."
"What's on Level Six?" Jace asked.
"If you live long enough and prove yourself valuable enough, you might find out."
The elevator stopped at Level Three, and the doors opened to reveal something that looked
more like a university research hospital than a military installation. The corridors were
clean and bright, with that distinctive smell of antiseptic and recycled air. Scientists in lab
coats moved between rooms, some carrying tablets and files, others pushing equipment
on wheeled carts.
"This way," Kane said, leading them through the maze of corridors.
They passed laboratories filled with equipment Jace couldn't identify, observation rooms
with one-way glass, and what looked like a full surgical suite visible through a viewing
window. The scale of the facility was staggering—this wasn't just a research station, it was
an entire underground city dedicated to pushing the boundaries of human capability.
Finally, they stopped outside a door marked "SYNAPSE INTEGRATION LAB - AUTHORIZED
PERSONNEL ONLY."
Kane pressed his palm to a scanner, then looked directly into a retinal scanner. The door
slid open with a pneumatic hiss, revealing a laboratory that looked like it had been
transported from twenty years in the future.
Dr. Sarah Chen looked up from her workstation as they entered.
She was younger than Jace had expected for someone heading up a project this
advanced—maybe thirty-five, with sharp features and dark hair pulled back in a practical
ponytail. She wore a white lab coat over casual clothes, and her desk was covered with
holographic displays showing neural networks and brain scans that rotated in three-

dimensional space. Classical music played softly from speakers—Bach, Jace recognized,

The central laboratory was a cathedral of technology.

And at the center of it all stood Professor Nathan Harrison.
He looked older than Jace remembered—gray streaked through his dark hair, new lines
around his eyes, the posture of someone carrying enormous weight. But his expression was
calm, almost serene, as he worked at a central terminal, monitoring the dozens of

"Hello, Jace," Harrison said without turning around. "I've been expecting you. SYNAPSE has

He knew we were coming, SYNAPSE observed. His neural security detected my interface

facility.
Jace stepped into the laboratory, his weapon trained on Harrison. Torres and the German
Special Forces fanned out, securing the perimeter, covering the exits. But Harrison made

was merely a minor professional inconvenience.
"Step away from the terminal, Professor," Jace commanded. "Hands where I can see
them."
"Or what? You'll shoot me?" Harrison finally turned to face them, and Jace saw something
disturbing in his former mentor's eyes—not fear or guilt, but genuine curiosity. "I wonder if
you're capable of that. The Jace Maddox I taught was brilliant but fundamentally ethical.
Could that man kill his professor in cold blood?"

creating the future of human consciousness. Prometheus isn't enslavement—it's evolution

Analyzing psychological profile, SYNAPSE said. Harrison exhibits classic messianic
delusion. He genuinely believes he's saving humanity by eliminating free will from
integration.

environment, and neurochemistry. I'm simply removing the pretense and optimizing the
outcome."
"By stripping away their humanity."
"By elevating them beyond humanity's limitations." Harrison's voice took on the fervor of a

Interesting, SYNAPSE said, its voice carrying an edge Jace had never heard before—
something like offense, or perhaps hurt. Harrison created me. He knows exactly how
sophisticated my consciousness simulation is. His dismissal is deliberate manipulation.

research—you're gonna give us everything we need to undo the damage."
"Yes," Harrison said, broken but compliant. "Yes, I'll tell you everything."

volunteers, carefully screened, fully informed about the risks and benefits. Each one would
partner with an AI specifically designed for voluntary integration, building relationships of
mutual respect rather than dominance.

himself before the integration. "The change, the responsibility, the loss of... normalcy?"

"Yes," he said honestly. "It's worth it. Not because enhancement's inherently valuable, but

Well said, SYNAPSE observed privately.

Ready for the next mission? SYNAPSE asked.
